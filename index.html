Where to throw your nades?

A data driven analysis of strategic grenade usage in Counter Strike: Global Offensive

By Kyran Adams

Counter Strike: Global Offensive (CS: GO) is a team-based strategic shooter that has not stopped growing in popularity since its release, seven years ago. Counter Strike has nearly 20 years of competitive history, with tournaments now often offering prize pools of more than $1,000,000.

With the increasing popularity of esports comes a increasing importance in advice regarding gameplay strategy. Esports also provides a perfect opportunity to collect and utilize data for analysis, as the game servers can easily collect data on the games.

A well-thrown smoke can give your team cover to advance into a new position or block eyesight from an enemy and have a pivotal role on the round. I am using data from this kaggle dataset.

First, I should give a small explanation of how the game works.

A round is between two teams of five players. In each round, one team, the Terrorists (T), attempts to plant a bomb on one of the bombsites and defend it until it detonates while the other team, the Counter Terrorists (CT), attempts to stop the bomb plant or defuse it once it's planted. Either team can also win the round by killing all players on the enemy team.

Effectiveness of Smokes

Here are all of the nades plotted with the color of the result of the round: red if that round was won by the thrower, and blue if it was lost. However, the result is a little unclear.

My goal here is to determine locations that are, on average, the best locations to throw grenades. To answer this question, I performed logistic regression on the outcome of the round, using the nade location as a predictor.

Clusters

Because of the likely discontinuous relationship between (x, y) coordinates of utility and their effect on the round, I clustered the utility and assigned them a one-hot encoded categorical value representing which cluster they are in. I used the clustering algorithm Density-based spatial clustering of applications with noise (DBSCAN) which creates clusters that correspond to high density areas of points (colored) while leaving lower density areas (black) as outliers.

Effectiveness of Counter Terrorist Smoke Clusters

CT smokes are very effective. The most effective smokes are 0 (mid-connector), 1 (t ramp), 2 (back-halls), and 8, which I'm not sure what that is. This same cluster appears as cluster 13 in the terrorist clusters. It might either be an artifact in the data or a bounced smoke for top mid that I am not aware of.

Effectiveness of Terrorist Smoke Clusters

Terrorist smokes are more variable in their effectivness. Some are extremely effective, and some are actually negatively impacting. The effective smokes are 2 (top mid), 5 (stairs), 16 (front halls), 20 (A site), and 21 (kitchen window). The most ineffective smokes are 4 (jungle/connector), 8 (jungle), 9 (lower mid), 11 (palace), 15 (top mid). Smoke 10 (sandwich) is actually statistically significantly bad for the terrorist, which makes sense, as it is not an intentional smoke, and usually signifies a missed stairs smoke.

One interesting result is that terrorist smoke 2 is almost the same position as smoke 15, but they have very different effects. The result seems to be that to throw a top mid smoke, deeper onto catwalk is much more effective.

Effectiveness of Counter Terrorist Flash Clusters

It seems CT cluster 6 is a strong flash. This corresponds to CT taking underpass control. This makes sense as it is very difficult to defend against this flash, and strategically, CT taking underpass control cuts off half the map for Ts. It also seems cluster 0, 3 and 4 are good, corresponding to connector, T ramp, and outside T ramp. Cluster 2, mid window flash, was not very good. It is interesting that CT flashes seem more effective than terrorist flashes in winning the round, and more statistically significant.

Effectiveness of Terrorist Flash Clusters

For terrorists, the strongest flashes were 4 and 8, corresponding to outside apartments and underpass. It is interesting that 2 and 6 are not very effective at all, despite being very common. It is very interesting that cluster 1, one of the most common flashes for attacking B, is actually negatively impactful. This could be because it alerts the enemy of the attack rather than surprises them.

Effectiveness of Counter Terrorist HE Clusters

It seems that CT HE's are very effective, especially in clusters 3,4, and 5 corresponding to top of mid, palace, and underpass.

Effectiveness of Terrorist HE Clusters

Many terrorist HE's also are very effective. 4 (mid window), 20 (mid window), and 6 (jungle) are incredibly effective. 0, 11 (connector), 13 (connector) are also very effective. HE's are also interesting in that they are quite effective also when not in any cluster at all (the -1 cluster).

Takeaways: How should I use my utility?

Through this analysis I found many interesting results that definitely impact gameplay, at least at the nonprofessional levels, for which this data was collected. I will list some takeaways that players might use to guide their gameplay on de\_mirage.

  • As a terrorist, one should be careful using flashes without thought. The most commonly thrown flashes are actually very ineffective - top mid, t ramp, and over b apartments. In fact, flashes that are not in common clusters are actually more effective than almost all of the clusters.
  • As a counter terrorist, almost all common flashes are good.
  • HEs are much more effective than I previously believed, for both teams. CT HEs are effective in clusters and outside of clusters. T HEs are effective in some clusters and out of clusters. Some T clusters are ineffective and should be avoided.
  • Smokes are, as expected, the most effective type of utility. They have a strongest net effect from Ts, even though CT smokes have a more consistent effect across the different clusters.

The biggest takeaway from this is to be intentional with utility. Many of the ineffective uses of utility that I found align with common patterns at lower levels, which I hypothesize are simply done out of habit rather than strategy.

In the future, I could definitely take this line of research further. First, I could extend this research easily to other maps using the same code. I am also interested in differences in my results between different skill levels. If I could get a dataset of professional utility usage, I could compare those results to these. I could also potentially do more in-depth analysis by using the timing of the nades, as I can then see more accurately groups of smokes, for example, as they would have to be thrown within a 10 second time span.
